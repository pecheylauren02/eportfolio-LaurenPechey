Network Security Management

This module explores key principles and best practices in managing network security within organisations. Topics include risk assessment, security policy development, threat identification, incident response, and the implementation of technical and organisational controls to protect network infrastructure.

UNIT 1

LEARNING OUTCOMES

  • Review the history of network security and how vulnerabilities have evolved.
  • Understand and discuss what constitutes a best practice approach to securing networks and systems.
  • Examine several modern scanning methods and attack techniques used by threat actors.

REFLECTION

Reflecting on my experience in the Network Security module Unit 1, I will utilise the Rolfe et al. (2001) reflective model to organise my thoughts and learning outcomes.

What?

This unit introduced me to the history of network security and how vulnerabilities have evolved over time. I learned how past breaches and threats have shaped modern security practices. We discussed the principles of best practice approaches to securing networks and systems, focusing on how layered defence and proactive management can help mitigate risks. The introduction to the Cyber Kill Chain model by Lockheed Martin (Hutchins et al., 2011) provided a structured way to analyse the stages of an attack and how each stage can be disrupted. This was linked to contemporary examples, including the SolarWinds exploit, helping me understand how theory translates to real-world breaches.

So What?

By reviewing common attacks and modern scanning and attack methods, I gained a clearer understanding of how threat actors exploit vulnerabilities. This awareness highlighted the importance of continuous monitoring and proactive defence. The best practice frameworks we discussed made me realise that network security is not static — it requires constant updates, user education, and the integration of robust tools and techniques. Analysing the Cyber Kill Chain made it easier to break down complex breaches into manageable parts, which I found helpful for planning mitigations and strengthening defences.

Now What?

Going forward, I plan to apply this knowledge by ensuring I can identify the origins of attacks and suggest realistic best practice approaches to mitigate them. I intend to use the Cyber Kill Chain model in future case studies or projects to break down and explain attacks in detail. I will also continue to keep up with new scanning tools and protection techniques, building on what I learned about modern attack methods. This unit has reinforced that staying informed and adopting a layered, proactive approach are essential for effective network security management.

UNIT 2

UML Modelling to Support Secure System Planning

Welcome to Week 2, during which we will gain some practical skills regarding the creation of flow charts. These are used during the design stage of the SDLC and can be used from that point onwards to support communications between stakeholders.

LEARNING OUTCOMES

  • Discuss the impact of Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs) and describe the Cyber Kill Chain model.
  • Apply the Cyber Kill Chain model to analyse a well-known APT, such as the SolarWinds exploit (Temple-Raston, 2021).
  • Evaluate possible mitigations to prevent or reduce the impact of similar exploits.

REFLECTION

Reflecting on my experience in the Network Security module Unit 2, I will use the Rolfe et al. (2001) reflective model to structure my learning and insights.

What?

In this unit, I revisited the Cyber Kill Chain model (Hutchins et al., 2011) and learned how to apply it to analyse a modern Advanced Persistent Threat (APT) case study — the SolarWinds exploit (Temple-Raston, 2021). This helped me understand how APTs operate through multiple attack stages, and how the Cyber Kill Chain breaks down these stages into identifiable phases. Studying the SolarWinds breach demonstrated how sophisticated and persistent modern attacks can be, highlighting the need for layered and proactive defences that address each step of an attack chain.

So What?

Applying the Cyber Kill Chain to a real-world example made me appreciate how theoretical models can support practical threat analysis. It also made me question the limitations of existing models when faced with increasingly complex APTs. I realised the importance of continuous evaluation of security frameworks to ensure they remain relevant. By analysing how the SolarWinds exploit was carried out and mitigated, I gained insights into the gaps that may exist in detection and response strategies, and how mitigations must adapt to evolving threats.

Now What?

Going forward, I plan to use the Cyber Kill Chain as a tool to break down attacks in future case studies and real-world scenarios. I will also stay informed about emerging APT techniques to test whether traditional models like the Cyber Kill Chain are sufficient or if new frameworks are needed. Understanding how to describe, analyse, and mitigate APTs will help me strengthen my approach to network security and ensure I consider both technical and organisational countermeasures. This unit reinforced that effective defence relies on combining practical models with up-to-date threat intelligence.

UNIT 3

Programming Languages: History, Concepts & Design

Welcome to Week 3 where we will spend some time investigating programming languages – looking at the history, concepts, and design that led to the languages we have today. Programming languages are a fundamental concept in computer science – they are the primary tool used by computer scientists and developers to design, define, create, and implement the tools we use today.

LEARNING OUTCOMES

  • Discuss different approaches to vulnerability testing and assessments, and select appropriate methods based on the target business.
  • Utilise open-source scanning and testing tools to perform vulnerability assessments.
  • Analyse assessment results and produce reports with recommendations to mitigate vulnerabilities.

REFLECTION

Reflecting on my experience in Unit 3 of the Network Security module, I will use the Rolfe et al. (2001) reflective model to structure my thoughts, with a focus on the Vulnerability Audit and Assessment for the Gin and Juice website.

What?

In this unit, I explored different approaches to vulnerability assessments, including Cyber Essentials, general vulnerability scans, and more advanced certified penetration tests. The lecturecast and readings gave me a clear framework for choosing suitable scanning tools, creating policies, and producing a baseline audit. The practical aspect of this unit was my Vulnerability Audit and Assessment assignment, which required me to assess the Gin and Juice website. I examined possible security vulnerabilities, identified relevant industry standards like GDPR, and noted areas of potential non-compliance. I also created a list of scanning and testing tools, justifying my choices, and proposed a realistic plan with recommendations prioritised by importance. This assignment accounts for 30% of the module grade and set the baseline for deeper analysis in future units.

So What?

This unit showed me how to connect theory to real-world practice. By conducting a baseline assessment of the Gin and Juice website, I gained valuable experience in analysing a live target and identifying realistic security challenges. I had to think critically about the assumptions and limitations of my chosen tools, as well as the potential operational impacts of running scans, such as scheduling out-of-hours scanning to minimise disruption. Structuring my findings clearly and concisely, with bullet points and tables, helped me stay within the word count while meeting the assignment requirements. This experience highlighted the importance of communicating technical findings in a way that a client can easily understand and act on.

Now What?

Moving forward, I will build on this experience by applying the baseline assessment approach to other projects, ensuring that I always link my analysis to appropriate industry standards. I also plan to expand my knowledge of scanning and testing tools, so I can better justify my choices in future reports. Producing clear timelines, listing assumptions and limitations, and presenting findings succinctly will be practices I carry into future vulnerability audits. This unit has shown me that setting a strong foundation with a well-documented baseline makes future in-depth testing and mitigation planning far more effective. Overall, this assignment reinforced my understanding of how to deliver a professional and valuable security assessment to a real client.

UNIT 4

LEARNING OUTCOMES

  • Review typical security breaches and describe common attack methods.
  • Suggest suitable mitigations to defend against these typical attacks.
  • Evaluate and select appropriate tools from the Kali Linux distribution for security testing.

REFLECTION

Reflecting on my experience in Unit 4 of the Network Security module, I will use the Rolfe et al. (2001) reflective model to structure my thoughts and learning points.

What?

This unit focused on analysing security breaches and formulating mitigation strategies using practical tools. I reviewed several typical security breaches to understand how attackers gain access, escalate privileges, and exploit vulnerabilities. The readings and activities highlighted the legal and ethical implications of breaches, including important points raised by Schwartz and Janger (2007) about organisations’ legal duties. A key practical component was exploring the Kali Linux distribution and its wide range of tools designed for security testing, penetration testing, and forensic analysis. Bhatt (2018) provided an overview of the most commonly used tools and their purposes, which I found useful for planning real-world security assessments.

So What?

This unit helped me connect the dots between theory and practice by showing how breaches are analysed in professional contexts. By studying real breaches, I understood how important it is to plan mitigations for each stage of an attack, from prevention to response and recovery. Working with Kali Linux introduced me to industry-standard tools and made me realise how vital it is to choose the right tool for each stage of an assessment. I also learned that simply having access to tools is not enough — using them responsibly and ethically, while considering legal implications, is critical for professional practice.

Now What?

Moving forward, I will expand my familiarity with the different tools in Kali Linux and practice applying them in realistic scenarios. I plan to build on this by using the tools to perform deeper breach analysis and mitigation planning in future units and projects. I will also pay close attention to the legal and ethical aspects of breach analysis, ensuring that any recommendations I make in my work are not only technically sound but also compliant with relevant laws and regulations. This unit has strengthened my ability to describe breach attacks, select appropriate mitigation strategies, and confidently use specialist tools to assess and respond to threats.

UNIT 5

LEARNING OUTCOMES

  • Review logging tools and best practices for Windows and Linux systems, and specify appropriate logging configurations.
  • Select and utilise forensic tools for data investigation.
  • Analyse the results obtained from log and forensic analysis tools.

REFLECTION

Reflecting on my experience in Unit 5 of the Network Security module, I will use the Rolfe et al. (2001) reflective model to organise my thoughts and learning outcomes.

What?

In this unit, I explored the role of logging and forensics in breach investigation and resolution. The lecturecast explained why logging is crucial for detecting, investigating, and responding to security incidents. I learned about logging configurations for both Windows and Linux systems, and how best practice differs across platforms. The unit also introduced common forensic tools and techniques, helping me understand what kind of evidence can be collected and analysed to identify the source and impact of a breach. The reading from Hertzog et al. (2017) provided practical advice on working with Kali Linux and reinforced my understanding of how logging and forensics fit into broader security assessments. I also reviewed a vulnerability assessment reporting template, which helped me link forensic findings back to reporting and documentation best practices.

So What?

This unit showed me how logging and forensic analysis provide the backbone for incident response. Understanding what to log, how to configure logs, and how to interpret them helps security professionals detect suspicious activity early. I also realised that forensic tools play a critical role in providing concrete evidence of an attack’s timeline, scope, and impact. The practical activity of reviewing the assessment reporting template helped me see how to translate technical findings into clear, actionable recommendations for clients or stakeholders. This unit tied together technical configuration skills with the need for clear communication and documentation, which is vital for legal and organisational follow-up after a breach.

Now What?

Going forward, I plan to build on this knowledge by practising how to configure logging systems for different environments and by testing various forensic tools on sample scenarios. I will focus on refining my ability to analyse log data and translate it into meaningful evidence. This includes understanding how security features like encryption or access controls can affect log interpretation. I also plan to develop my skills in creating clear, comprehensive reports that present forensic findings and mitigation steps effectively. This unit has strengthened my understanding of how logging and forensics support the full lifecycle of network security — from prevention to investigation and response.
